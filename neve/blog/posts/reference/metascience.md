## Epistemology

### Meaningness — David Chapman

*Web book* — [meaningness.com](https://meaningness.com)

Chapman's companion project to *In the Cells of the Eggplant*, focused on how people understand meaning, purpose, and value. The thesis: meaning is *nebulous yet patterned* — meaningfulness and meaninglessness intermingle, and the central errors are eternalism (treating meaning as fixed and absolute) and nihilism (denying it exists at all). Both are escape hatches from the discomfort of genuine nebulosity. The book maps out "stances" — emotional-intellectual postures people take toward meaning — and traces how cultural meaning-systems have evolved historically. Practical rather than purely theoretical; the aim is frameworks that reduce unnecessary suffering and support more effective engagement with existential questions. Ongoing and available by subscription.

### In the Cells of the Eggplant — David Chapman

*Web book* — [metarationality.com](https://metarationality.com)

Chapman's ongoing book-length project on meta-rationality — how to use rational systems more effectively by understanding their relationship with surrounding context. The central problem: formal rationality runs into *nebulosity* (real-world fuzziness, vagueness, context-dependence) and cannot on its own provide the certainty it promises. Meta-rationality is the capacity to evaluate, select, modify, and create rational methods rather than being trapped inside any one of them. Covers the failures of logical positivism, the limits of probability theory and statistics, the nature of definitions and reference, and what "everyday reasonableness" contributes that formal systems miss. Two of five planned parts are currently complete. Chapman notes it is not suited to reading in pieces — the conceptual structure is dense and sequential.

### A First Lesson in Meta-Rationality — David Chapman (2016)

*Article* — [metarationality.com](https://metarationality.com/bongard-meta-rationality)

Chapman uses Bongard problems — visual puzzles where you must *discover* the rule rather than apply a known one — as a vehicle for introducing meta-rationality: the capacity to evaluate, modify, and create systems of reasoning rather than merely operate within them. Solving Bongard problems requires shifting vocabulary and levels of description dynamically, filtering what's relevant from what isn't, with no fixed procedure to follow. Chapman positions this as "stage 5" cognition, succeeding the systematic/rational mode (stage 4). The key epistemological point: concepts have inherent vagueness, and advanced technical work lives in the space where you have to navigate that vagueness rather than pretend it away — the failure modes being eternalism (false precision) and nihilism (abandoning rigor altogether).

## Cargo Cult Science

### Cargo Cult Science — Richard Feynman (1974)

*Article* — [calteches.library.caltech.edu](https://calteches.library.caltech.edu/51/2/CargoCult.htm)

Feynman's Caltech commencement address, the origin of the term. South Seas islanders built wooden runways and control towers after WWII, mimicking the forms that had brought aircraft — the form was perfect, nothing worked. Feynman's argument: vast swaths of research (psychology, education, parapsychology) do the same — they copy the procedures of science without capturing its essence. The missing ingredient is *scientific integrity*: the utter honesty that requires you to report everything that might contradict your result. "The first principle is that you must not fool yourself — and you are the easiest person to fool."

### Upgrade Your Cargo Cult — David Chapman (2016)

*Article* — [metarationality.com](https://metarationality.com/upgrade-your-cargo-cult)

Chapman extends Feynman's diagnosis from pseudoscience to mainstream institutions — science, medicine, business, governance. Most operate as cargo cults: scripted busy-work justified by ideologies that have lost contact with reality. Chapman adds a developmental layer: learning requires ability → understanding → representation in sequence; imitation is necessary but insufficient without legitimate peripheral participation, honest engagement with phenomena, and epistemic virtues (curiosity, courage, caring about the subject). The upgrade from cargo cult to genuine practice is what he calls meta-rational competence — knowing when methods work, when they fail, and how to adapt.
