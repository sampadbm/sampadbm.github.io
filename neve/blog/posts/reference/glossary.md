## Loewner Order

A partial ordering on symmetric (or Hermitian) matrices where $A \succeq B$ if and only if $A - B$ is positive semi-definite — essentially a way to say one matrix is "greater than or equal to" another by generalizing the notion of $\geq$ from scalars to matrices.

## Partial Trace

A linear map $\mathrm{Tr}_W : \mathcal{L}(V \otimes W) \to \mathcal{L}(V)$ that "traces out" one factor of a tensor product space, generalizing the scalar-valued trace $\mathrm{Tr} : \mathcal{L}(V) \to \mathbb{C}$ to an operator-valued quantity. On product operators it acts by $\mathrm{Tr}_W(R \otimes S) = \mathrm{Tr}(S)\, R$, and this together with linearity determines it uniquely and independently of basis. Concretely, expanding $T \in \mathcal{L}(V \otimes W)$ in a basis $\{|k\rangle\}$ of $W$ as $T = \sum_{k,\ell} T^{(k\ell)} \otimes |k\rangle\langle\ell|$ gives $\mathrm{Tr}_W(T) = \sum_j T^{(jj)}$ — one sums the diagonal $W$-blocks while keeping the $V$-structure intact. **Quantum role:** if a composite system has density matrix $\rho_{AB}$ on $\mathcal{H}_A \otimes \mathcal{H}_B$, the reduced density matrix $\rho_A = \mathrm{Tr}_B(\rho_{AB})$ describes the state of subsystem $A$ alone — it is the unique operator on $\mathcal{H}_A$ that reproduces the correct expectation values $\mathrm{Tr}(\rho_A \, M_A) = \mathrm{Tr}(\rho_{AB}\, (M_A \otimes I_B))$ for every local observable $M_A$. This is the quantum analogue of marginalizing a joint probability distribution. When $\rho_{AB}$ is a pure entangled state, $\rho_A$ is generally *mixed*, and the Schmidt decomposition makes this explicit: the eigenvalues of $\rho_A$ and $\rho_B$ coincide (up to zeros). **Properties:** the partial trace is completely positive and trace-preserving (a CPTP map / quantum channel), and it commutes with the full trace in the sense $\mathrm{Tr}(\mathrm{Tr}_W(T)) = \mathrm{Tr}(T)$. Central to entanglement measures (von Neumann entropy $S(\rho_A)$), open quantum systems (system–environment partitioning), and quantum error correction.

## Tensor Product of Hilbert Spaces

A construction that combines two Hilbert spaces $\mathcal{H}_1$ and $\mathcal{H}_2$ into a new Hilbert space $\mathcal{H}_1 \otimes \mathcal{H}_2$ by taking the algebraic tensor product, equipping it with the inner product $\langle u_1 \otimes u_2, v_1 \otimes v_2 \rangle = \langle u_1, v_1 \rangle \langle u_2, v_2 \rangle$, and completing the space — used extensively in quantum mechanics to describe composite systems.

## Universal Property

A way of defining a mathematical object by specifying how it relates to all other objects of a given kind — if an object $X$ satisfies a universal property, then any other object doing the same thing must factor through $X$ uniquely, characterizing $X$ up to unique isomorphism. This is the core idea behind constructions like products, coproducts, tensor products, and free objects in category theory.

## Darboux Sum

For a bounded function $f : [a, b] \to \mathbb{R}$ and a partition $P = \{a = x_0 < x_1 < \dots < x_n = b\}$, the **lower** and **upper** Darboux sums are
$$L(f, P) = \sum_{i=1}^n m_i (x_i - x_{i-1}), \qquad U(f, P) = \sum_{i=1}^n M_i (x_i - x_{i-1}),$$
where $m_i = \inf_{[x_{i-1}, x_i]} f$ and $M_i = \sup_{[x_{i-1}, x_i]} f$. Geometrically, $L(f,P)$ is the total area of the tallest rectangles fitting *under* the graph on each subinterval, and $U(f,P)$ is the area of the shortest rectangles covering it *from above*. Refining $P$ makes $L$ non-decreasing and $U$ non-increasing. The **lower** and **upper Darboux integrals** are $\underline{\int_a^b} f = \sup_P L(f,P)$ and $\overline{\int_a^b} f = \inf_P U(f,P)$; $f$ is **Darboux-integrable** when they agree, and the common value is $\int_a^b f$. **Relation to Riemann sums.** A Riemann sum picks an arbitrary sample point in each subinterval, while Darboux picks the supremum / infimum — so every Riemann sum is sandwiched $L \leq \sum f(\xi_i)\Delta x_i \leq U$. Darboux and Riemann integrability are *equivalent* (same class of functions, same value), but Darboux's formulation is usually easier to reason with: it replaces the quantifier "for every choice of sample points" with a cleaner sup/inf, making proofs of properties like linearity, monotonicity, and the Cauchy criterion (integrability iff $U(f,P) - L(f,P)$ can be made arbitrarily small) nearly mechanical.

## Integral of Inverse Functions

If $f : [a, b] \to [c, d]$ is continuous and strictly monotonic (so $f^{-1}$ exists), with $f(a) = c$ and $f(b) = d$, then
$$\int_a^b f(x)\, dx \;+\; \int_c^d f^{-1}(y)\, dy \;=\; bd - ac.$$
Equivalently, with $F$ any antiderivative of $f$, an antiderivative of $f^{-1}$ is $\int f^{-1}(y)\,dy = y f^{-1}(y) - F(f^{-1}(y)) + C$ — a useful identity when $f^{-1}$ has no elementary antiderivative but $f$ does (e.g., $\int \log y\, dy$ from $\int e^x\, dx$, or $\int \arcsin y\, dy$ from $\int \sin x\, dx$). **Proof without words.** The identity is the quintessential proof-without-words: in the $xy$-plane, the region under the graph of $f$ from $a$ to $b$ and the region under the graph of $f^{-1}$ from $c$ to $d$ fit together — *along the curve itself* — to tile the rectangle $[0,b] \times [0,d]$ minus $[0,a]\times[0,c]$, whose area is exactly $bd - ac$. Seen as areas, the two integrals are complementary slices of the same rectangle across the diagonal $y = f(x)$. **History.** Published by Charles-Ange Laisant (1905), independently rediscovered several times (Caprilli 1912, Parker 1955, Key 1994 for a Darboux-integral version without differentiability). **Connection to Young's inequality / Legendre transform.** Generalizing: for monotonically increasing $f$ with $f(0) = 0$, Young's inequality $ab \leq \int_0^a f(x)\, dx + \int_0^b f^{-1}(y)\, dy$ (equality iff $b = f(a)$) is the convex-analysis shadow of this identity, and the Legendre transform $f^*(p) = \sup_x(px - f(x))$ formalizes the same "swap of axes" systematically.

## Legendre Transformation

An involutive transform that converts a convex function $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ into its **convex conjugate** (a.k.a. Fenchel conjugate)
$$f^*(p) \;=\; \sup_{x \in \mathrm{dom}(f)} \bigl( p^\top x - f(x) \bigr).$$
For differentiable strictly convex $f$ the supremum is attained at the unique $x$ solving $\nabla f(x) = p$, and $f^*(p) = p^\top x(p) - f(x(p))$. **Geometric picture.** Parameterizing $f$ by *slope* instead of *position*: $-f^*(p)$ is the $y$-intercept of the tangent line to $y = f(x)$ whose slope is $p$, so $f^*$ encodes the same function as an envelope of tangent lines. **Involution.** For convex lower-semicontinuous $f$, $f^{**} = f$ — the transform is self-inverse. (For non-convex $f$, $f^{**}$ is the convex envelope, the largest convex minorant.) **Derivative relation.** $\nabla f^*$ and $\nabla f$ are mutual inverses as maps between "primal" and "dual" coordinates: $p = \nabla f(x) \iff x = \nabla f^*(p)$. This is the multivariate generalization of the one-dimensional integral-of-inverse-functions identity — the conjugate's derivative is the inverse of the original's derivative. **Young–Fenchel inequality.** $f(x) + f^*(p) \geq x^\top p$ for all $x, p$, with equality iff $p = \nabla f(x)$ (equivalently $x \in \partial f^*(p)$). Setting $f(x) = |x|^p/p$ for $p > 1$ recovers the classical Young inequality with $f^*(q) = |q|^{p'}/p'$ and $1/p + 1/p' = 1$. **Applications.** (i) *Analytical mechanics:* Hamiltonian $H(q, p)$ is the Legendre transform of the Lagrangian $L(q, \dot q)$ in the velocity variable, swapping $\dot q \leftrightarrow p$. (ii) *Thermodynamics:* internal energy $U(S, V)$ transforms to Helmholtz $F(T, V) = U - TS$, Gibbs $G(T, p)$, enthalpy $H(S, p)$ — each Legendre transform swapping an extensive variable for its conjugate intensive one. (iii) *Convex optimization:* Lagrangian duality, Fenchel–Rockafellar duality ($\inf f(Ax) + g(x) = \sup -f^*(-y) - g^*(A^\top y)$), proximal operators, entropy regularization (log-partition function and its dual in max-ent / exponential-family modeling). (iv) *Information theory:* the KL divergence and cumulant-generating function / rate function pairs in large deviations are Legendre-dual.

## Young's Inequality for Products

For non-negative reals $a, b \geq 0$ and *conjugate exponents* $p, q > 1$ satisfying $\tfrac{1}{p} + \tfrac{1}{q} = 1$,
$$ab \;\leq\; \frac{a^p}{p} + \frac{b^q}{q},$$
with equality iff $a^p = b^q$. **Proofs.** (i) *Concavity of log:* write $ab = \exp(\tfrac{1}{p}\log a^p + \tfrac{1}{q}\log b^q)$ and apply Jensen's inequality to the concave $\log$. (ii) *Geometric / integral form:* the rectangle $[0,a] \times [0,b]$ is covered by the union of the region under $y = x^{p-1}$ from $0$ to $a$ (area $a^p/p$) and the region under its inverse $x = y^{q-1}$ from $0$ to $b$ (area $b^q/q$) — this is literally the integral-of-inverse-functions picture specialized to a power function. (iii) *Legendre transform:* for $f(x) = x^p/p$ the convex conjugate is $f^*(y) = y^q/q$ (precisely when $1/p + 1/q = 1$), and Young's inequality is the Fenchel inequality $ab \leq f(a) + f^*(b)$. **Role in analysis.** It is the workhorse lemma used to prove **Hölder's inequality** $\int |fg| \leq \|f\|_p \|g\|_q$ (apply pointwise after normalization), which in turn gives the duality of $L^p$ and $L^q$, the triangle inequality for $L^p$ norms (Minkowski), and interpolation results like Riesz–Thorin. **Peter–Paul variant** ("Young's inequality with $\varepsilon$"): for any $\varepsilon > 0$,
$$ab \;\leq\; \frac{a^2}{2\varepsilon} + \frac{\varepsilon b^2}{2},$$
a parameterized bound used ubiquitously in PDE a priori estimates to absorb a "bad" cross-term into a coercive quadratic on one side — the name captures the tradeoff of robbing one term's control to pay off another.

## K-SVD

A dictionary learning algorithm that iteratively learns an overcomplete dictionary $D$ and sparse codes $X$ to represent data as $Y \approx DX$, by alternating between sparse coding (e.g. via OMP) and updating each dictionary atom using a rank-1 SVD approximation — a generalization of K-means where each signal can use multiple atoms instead of just one.

## Sparse Approximation

The problem of representing a signal $y$ as a linear combination of as few columns as possible from a (typically overcomplete) dictionary $D$, i.e. finding the sparsest $x$ such that $y \approx Dx$ — solved approximately via methods like matching pursuit, OMP, or basis pursuit ($\ell_1$ relaxation).

## Sparse Dictionary Learning

The problem of jointly learning a dictionary $D$ *and* sparse codes $X$ from data $Y$, unlike sparse approximation which assumes a fixed dictionary — the learned dictionary adapts to the data and typically yields much sparser representations than predefined bases like Fourier or wavelets. Algorithms include K-SVD, MOD, and online dictionary learning.

## Sparse Autoencoder (SAE)

An autoencoder with a sparsity constraint on the hidden layer activations — by penalizing the network so that only a few neurons activate for any given input, it learns a compressed, interpretable overcomplete representation. Recently used in mechanistic interpretability to decompose neural network activations into human-readable features.

## Sparse PCA

A variant of PCA that adds a sparsity constraint (e.g. $\ell_1$ penalty) on the loadings so that each principal component depends on only a few original variables — trading off some explained variance for much more interpretable components.

## Nonlinear Eigenproblem

A generalization of the standard eigenvalue problem $Ax = \lambda x$ to $M(\lambda)x = 0$, where the matrix $M$ depends nonlinearly on the eigenvalue $\lambda$ — arises naturally in vibration analysis, stability problems, and delay differential equations.

## Generalized Eigenvalue Problem

The problem of finding scalar $\lambda$ and vector $x$ satisfying $Ax = \lambda Bx$ for two matrices $A$ and $B$ — reduces to the standard eigenvalue problem when $B = I$. Common in mechanics (mass and stiffness matrices), Fisher's discriminant analysis, and canonical correlation analysis.

## Quadratic Eigenvalue Problem

Finding $\lambda$ and $x$ satisfying $(\lambda^2 M + \lambda C + K)x = 0$ — a special case of the nonlinear eigenproblem where the matrix depends quadratically on $\lambda$. Arises in damped vibration systems where $M$, $C$, and $K$ are the mass, damping, and stiffness matrices respectively.

## Bonnet's and Price's Theorems (Gaussian-Expectation Gradients)

Two identities for differentiating a Gaussian expectation with respect to its parameters — the workhorse tools for **Gaussian variational inference** and related parameter-space gradient methods in probabilistic ML. Let $q(x) = \mathcal{N}(x;\mu,\Sigma)$ and $f : \mathbb{R}^n \to \mathbb{R}$ be sufficiently smooth with enough tail decay.

**Bonnet's theorem (gradient w.r.t. the mean).**
$$\nabla_\mu\,\mathbb{E}_{q}[f(x)] \;=\; \mathbb{E}_{q}\!\left[\nabla_x f(x)\right].$$
Pulls the mean-gradient through the expectation as the expectation of the gradient — a one-line consequence of $\partial_{\mu_i} q(x) = -\partial_{x_i} q(x)$ (visible from the Gaussian density's dependence on $x - \mu$), followed by integration by parts.

**Price's theorem (gradient w.r.t. the covariance).**
$$\nabla_\Sigma\,\mathbb{E}_{q}[f(x)] \;=\; \tfrac{1}{2}\,\mathbb{E}_{q}\!\left[\nabla^2_x f(x)\right].$$
Covariance-gradient equals half the expected Hessian — follows from $\partial_{\Sigma_{ij}} q(x) = \tfrac{1}{2}\,\partial^2_{x_i x_j} q(x)$ (a direct calculation from the Gaussian density) plus integration by parts twice. Component-wise, $\partial\,\mathbb{E}_q[f]/\partial \Sigma_{ij} = \tfrac{1}{2}\,\mathbb{E}_q[\partial^2 f/\partial x_i\,\partial x_j]$.

**Why they matter.**
- **Gaussian VI / ELBO gradients.** When the variational family is $q_\phi = \mathcal{N}(\mu,\Sigma)$ and the target $f(x) = \log p(x,\mathcal{D}) - \log q_\phi(x)$ is differentiable, Bonnet and Price together give $\nabla_\phi\,\mathrm{ELBO}$ in closed form — no sampling, no score-function estimator variance. They are the analytic counterpart to the reparameterization trick and produce the same gradients.
- **Natural-evolution strategies.** Parameter-space gradient estimators in black-box optimization use exactly these identities to update a Gaussian search distribution from function evaluations.
- **Stein-type identities.** Bonnet is the multivariate analogue of **Stein's lemma** $\mathbb{E}[(X-\mu)f(X)] = \sigma^2\,\mathbb{E}[f'(X)]$ (scalar case); Price is its second-order analogue. Both are instances of integration by parts against the Gaussian density.
- **History.** Price (1958) introduced the covariance-derivative identity in a signal-processing context (correlations at the output of a nonlinear device driven by Gaussian input); Bonnet's mean-derivative companion is named for Georges Bonnet in the same tradition. Murphy's *Probabilistic Machine Learning* book uses them together to derive Gaussian-VI gradients.

**Tail conditions.** Both identities require $f$ and its derivatives to grow subexponentially at infinity so that the boundary terms in integration by parts vanish — for bounded or polynomial-growth $f$ this is automatic.

## Preconditioning

$$Ax = b \implies MAx = Mb, \quad M \approx A^{-1}$$

$MA$ has a lower condition number than $A$, so iterative methods converge faster. But a better $M$ means more expensive to compute — at the limit $M = A^{-1}$ you've already solved the problem, so you're always paying part of the cost you were trying to avoid. A common example is ILU (Incomplete LU factorization), which computes an approximate $LU$ decomposition of $A$ by dropping small fill-in entries to keep it sparse, then uses $M = (LU)^{-1}$ as the preconditioner.

## Krylov Subspace

Given $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ and $b \in \mathbb{R}^n$, the order-$k$ **Krylov subspace** is

$$\mathcal{K}_k(A, b) = \operatorname{span}\{b, Ab, A^2 b, \ldots, A^{k-1} b\}.$$

It is the natural search space for iterative methods that only access $A$ through matrix-vector products — you never form $A^{-1}$, you just keep multiplying by $A$ and take the best approximation inside $\mathcal{K}_k$. The canonical algorithms are all projections onto $\mathcal{K}_k$ under different optimality criteria: **Conjugate Gradient** (SPD $A$, minimize the $A$-norm of the error), **MINRES** (symmetric $A$, minimize $\|b - Ax\|_2$), **GMRES** (general $A$, same residual minimization via Arnoldi), **Lanczos** (symmetric eigenvalue approximation), and **Arnoldi** (nonsymmetric eigenvalue approximation). The SVD / eigendecomposition approximations these produce are exactly how **randomized SVD** and block-Krylov sketches get their accuracy guarantees — $\mathcal{K}_k(A, \Omega)$ for a random start $\Omega$ captures the dominant singular subspace of $A$ in $k$ matvecs.

Three facts worth keeping in hand. **(1) Nested and $A$-invariant in the limit:** $\mathcal{K}_1 \subseteq \mathcal{K}_2 \subseteq \cdots$ stabilizes once $\mathcal{K}_k = \mathcal{K}_{k+1}$, at which point it is an $A$-invariant subspace containing $b$ — this is the grade of $b$ with respect to $A$, and equals the degree of the minimal polynomial of $A$ restricted to $b$. **(2) Polynomial characterization:** $x \in \mathcal{K}_k(A, b)$ iff $x = p(A) b$ for some polynomial $p$ of degree $< k$, so Krylov methods are equivalently a search over degree-$(k-1)$ polynomials in $A$, and convergence rates follow from polynomial approximation bounds on the spectrum of $A$ (Chebyshev for CG, the key reason clustering eigenvalues via preconditioning accelerates convergence). **(3) Ill-conditioning of the raw basis:** $\{b, Ab, A^2 b, \ldots\}$ numerically collapses to the dominant eigenvector — the power iteration effect — so implementations orthogonalize on the fly (Arnoldi / Lanczos recurrences) rather than working in the monomial basis.

Closely related to **Preconditioning** above: a good preconditioner $M \approx A^{-1}$ works by replacing the spectrum of $A$ with the much more clustered spectrum of $MA$, shrinking the polynomial-approximation problem that governs Krylov convergence.

## Successive Over-Relaxation (SOR)

Classical stationary iterative solver for $Ax = b$. Split $A = D + L + U$ into diagonal, strict-lower, and strict-upper parts. **Jacobi** uses $x^{(k+1)} = D^{-1}(b - (L+U)x^{(k)})$; **Gauss–Seidel** uses the most recent iterates in place, $(D+L)x^{(k+1)} = b - U x^{(k)}$. **SOR** extrapolates each Gauss–Seidel update by a relaxation parameter $\omega \in (0, 2)$:

$$x_i^{(k+1)} = (1 - \omega)\, x_i^{(k)} + \frac{\omega}{a_{ii}}\left(b_i - \sum_{j < i} a_{ij} x_j^{(k+1)} - \sum_{j > i} a_{ij} x_j^{(k)}\right).$$

In matrix form, $(D + \omega L)\, x^{(k+1)} = \omega b + [(1-\omega) D - \omega U]\, x^{(k)}$. $\omega = 1$ recovers Gauss–Seidel; $\omega > 1$ is **over-relaxation** (accelerates convergence on well-conditioned SPD systems), $\omega < 1$ is **under-relaxation** (stabilizes otherwise-divergent iterations). Convergence requires the spectral radius of the iteration matrix $M_\omega = (D + \omega L)^{-1}[(1-\omega) D - \omega U]$ to be $< 1$; **Kahan's theorem** gives the necessary condition $\omega \in (0, 2)$, and for SPD $A$ this is also sufficient (**Ostrowski–Reich**).

For consistently-ordered matrices (the model case being 5-point finite-difference Laplacians), **Young's theorem** gives the optimal $\omega^\star = 2/(1 + \sqrt{1 - \rho_J^2})$ where $\rho_J$ is the Jacobi spectral radius, and the resulting asymptotic convergence factor is $\omega^\star - 1$ — an order-of-magnitude speedup over Gauss–Seidel when $\rho_J \to 1$. Largely displaced as a standalone solver by Krylov methods and multigrid, but still alive as a **smoother** inside multigrid (relax high-frequency error modes cheaply) and as a **preconditioner** ($M = (D + \omega L) D^{-1} (D + \omega U)/\omega(2-\omega)$ is the symmetric SOR / SSOR preconditioner used with CG).

## Symmetric → Normal → Commuting Family

A chain of increasingly general diagonalizability results. **Symmetric:** $[A, A^T] = 0$ implies there exists an orthonormal basis of eigenvectors. **Normal:** $[A, A^*] = 0$ implies there exists a unitary matrix that diagonalizes $A$. **Commuting family:** $[A_i, A_j] = 0$ for all normal $A_i, A_j$ implies there exists a single unitary matrix that simultaneously diagonalizes all of them.

## Total Sum of Squares (TSS / SST)

The total variability of a response variable around its mean, $\mathrm{TSS} = \sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - \bar y)^2$, where $\bar y = \frac{1}{n}\sum_i y_i$. It is $(n-1)$ times the sample variance of $y$, and serves as the baseline "how much is there to explain" against which model fit is measured. **Fundamental decomposition** (for any linear model fit with an intercept so that residuals have zero mean and are uncorrelated with fitted values):
$$\underbrace{\sum_i (y_i - \bar y)^2}_{\mathrm{TSS}} \;=\; \underbrace{\sum_i (\hat y_i - \bar y)^2}_{\mathrm{ESS \; (explained)}} \;+\; \underbrace{\sum_i (y_i - \hat y_i)^2}_{\mathrm{RSS \; (residual)}}.$$
This is the Pythagorean decomposition in $\mathbb{R}^n$ of the centered response into its projection onto the column space of the design (minus the constant) and the orthogonal residual. It underlies the **coefficient of determination** $R^2 = \mathrm{ESS}/\mathrm{TSS} = 1 - \mathrm{RSS}/\mathrm{TSS}$ — the fraction of variability accounted for by the model. **In ANOVA:** TSS splits into between-group and within-group sums of squares, and the $F$-statistic is a ratio of mean squares formed from these pieces. **In MANOVA** the scalar decomposition generalizes to a matrix identity $T = B + W$ on sum-of-squares-and-products (SSP) matrices. **Caveat:** the decomposition $\mathrm{TSS} = \mathrm{ESS} + \mathrm{RSS}$ requires an intercept (or that $\hat y$ and residuals are mean-consistent); for regression through the origin it fails, and $R^2$ defined this way can be negative or exceed $1$.

## Sum of Squares and Products (SSP) Matrix

The multivariate generalization of the sum of squares: where a scalar response gives $\sum_i (y_i - \bar y)^2 \in \mathbb{R}$, a vector response $y_i \in \mathbb{R}^q$ gives the **total SSP matrix** $T = \sum_i (y_i - \bar y)(y_i - \bar y)^\top \in \mathbb{R}^{q \times q}$ — the (unnormalized) sample covariance, with diagonal entries the per-dimension sums of squares and off-diagonals the sums of cross-products. In **MANOVA** the scalar ANOVA identity $\mathrm{TSS} = \mathrm{SS}_{\mathrm{between}} + \mathrm{SS}_{\mathrm{within}}$ lifts verbatim to matrices:
$$T \;=\; B \;+\; W,$$
where $W = \sum_g \sum_{i \in g}(y_{gi} - \bar y_g)(y_{gi} - \bar y_g)^\top$ is the **within-groups** SSP matrix (pooled group-centered scatter) and $B = \sum_g n_g (\bar y_g - \bar y)(\bar y_g - \bar y)^\top$ is the **between-groups** SSP matrix (cf. [Total sum of squares — MANOVA partition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Total_sum_of_squares)). The partition is a two-line bookkeeping identity: write $y_{gi} - \bar y = (y_{gi} - \bar y_g) + (\bar y_g - \bar y)$, square out the outer product, and note that the cross-term $\sum_{i \in g}(y_{gi} - \bar y_g)(\bar y_g - \bar y)^\top$ vanishes for each group because $\sum_{i \in g}(y_{gi} - \bar y_g) = 0$. With $N$ total observations and $K$ groups, the associated **degrees of freedom** mirror scalar ANOVA exactly: $\mathrm{df}(T) = N - 1$, $\mathrm{df}(B) = K - 1$, $\mathrm{df}(W) = N - K$.

**MANOVA test statistics.** Wilks' $\Lambda = |W|/|T|$, Pillai's trace $\mathrm{tr}(B(B+W)^{-1})$, Lawley–Hotelling $\mathrm{tr}(W^{-1}B)$, and Roy's largest root are all invariants of the generalized eigenvalue problem $B v = \lambda W v$ — they are different symmetric functions of the $\lambda_i$'s, so they differ in power against different alternatives but agree under the null. Up to scaling, $T/(N-1) = \hat\Sigma$ is the pooled **sample covariance** and $W/(N-K) = \hat\Sigma_W$ is the **pooled within-class covariance** — the workhorse estimators for multivariate normal-theory inference, LDA (which uses $\hat\Sigma_W$), and QDA (which uses per-class $W_g/(n_g-1)$ instead of pooling). The **two-group special case** ($K=2$) collapses Roy's root to **Hotelling's $T^2$ statistic**,
$$T^2 \;=\; \tfrac{n_1 n_2}{n_1 + n_2}\,(\bar y_1 - \bar y_2)^\top \hat\Sigma_W^{-1} (\bar y_1 - \bar y_2),$$
i.e., a squared **Mahalanobis distance** between group means, tying MANOVA, LDA, Hotelling's test, and Mahalanobis geometry into one family.

**Connection to LDA.** Fisher's linear discriminant analysis uses *the same* $B$ and $W$ matrices (terminology: within- and between-groups SSP / scatter matrices) and seeks a projection direction $w \in \mathbb{R}^q$ that maximizes the **Fisher criterion** — the ratio of between-class to within-class variance after projection:
$$w^* \;=\; \arg\max_{w} \frac{w^\top B w}{w^\top W w}.$$
The first-order conditions give the generalized eigenvalue problem $B w = \lambda W w$, equivalently $W^{-1}B\, w = \lambda w$ when $W$ is invertible. The top $\min(q, K-1)$ eigenvectors span the **discriminant subspace** onto which LDA projects for classification or visualization. So MANOVA and LDA are two uses of the same decomposition $T = B + W$: MANOVA *tests* whether $B$ is large relative to $W$ (group means differ), while LDA *extracts* the directions in which it is largest (most discriminative features).

**Multivariate regression SSP.** For a matrix-valued linear model $Y = XB + E$ with $Y \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times q}$, $X \in \mathbb{R}^{N \times p}$, the OLS fit $\hat B = (X^\top X)^{-1} X^\top Y$ yields the **residual SSP** $E^\top E = (Y - X\hat B)^\top(Y - X\hat B)$ and the **regression SSP** $\hat Y^\top M_0 \hat Y$ (with $M_0$ the centering projector), so the scalar Pythagorean identity $\mathrm{TSS} = \mathrm{ESS} + \mathrm{RSS}$ lifts directly to matrices $T = \mathrm{ESS}_{\mathrm{mat}} + E^\top E$. This closes the loop with the scalar sum-of-squares cluster above (TSS / ESS / RSS): the MANOVA partition is just the group-means special case where $X$ encodes class indicators.

**Rank and singularity.** $B$ has rank at most $K - 1$ (built from $K$ group-mean deviations that sum to zero), and $W$ has rank at most $\min(q, N - K)$. The latter is the constraint that bites in the **high-dimensional regime** $q > N - K$: $W$ is singular, $W^{-1}$ does not exist, Wilks' $\Lambda$ and Hotelling's trace are undefined, and vanilla LDA collapses. Standard fixes — **regularized LDA (RDA)** using $W + \lambda I$, **shrinkage covariance estimators** (Ledoit–Wolf) interpolating between $W$ and a diagonal target, and **subspace / nullspace LDA** that projects into $\mathrm{range}(W)$ first — restore invertibility and recover the same generalized eigenstructure in the reduced space. The Loewner-order interlacing of $W$ and $W + \lambda I$ (Cauchy interlacing applied to $B v = \lambda W v$) tells you exactly how $\lambda$ biases the discriminant directions.

## Explained Sum of Squares (ESS)

The portion of total response variability accounted for by a fitted model, $\mathrm{ESS} = \sum_{i=1}^n (\hat y_i - \bar y)^2$ — the spread of the *fitted* values around the mean of the *observed* values. In OLS with intercept and design $X = [\mathbf{1} \; \tilde X]$, let $H = X(X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top$ be the hat matrix and $M_0 = I - \tfrac{1}{n}\mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^\top$ the centering projector. Then $\hat y = Hy$, $\bar y \mathbf{1} = \tfrac{1}{n}\mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^\top y$, and the identity
$$y^\top M_0 y \;=\; y^\top (H - \tfrac{1}{n}\mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^\top) y \;+\; y^\top (I - H) y$$
(i.e. $\mathrm{TSS} = \mathrm{ESS} + \mathrm{RSS}$) follows because $H$ and $I - H$ are orthogonal projectors *and* $H\mathbf{1} = \mathbf{1}$ when an intercept is present, so $\tfrac{1}{n}\mathbf{1}\mathbf{1}^\top$ lies entirely inside $H$. The **intercept requirement is essential**: without it, $H\mathbf{1} \neq \mathbf{1}$ in general, residuals need not average to zero, $X^\top e = 0$ does not force $\mathbf{1}^\top e = 0$, and the neat orthogonal split breaks — one can even have $\mathrm{ESS} > \mathrm{TSS}$, making $R^2 = \mathrm{ESS}/\mathrm{TSS}$ exceed $1$ or turn negative when defined as $1 - \mathrm{RSS}/\mathrm{TSS}$. In matrix form $\mathrm{ESS} = \hat\beta^\top X^\top M_0 X \hat\beta$, and in the simple bivariate case it reduces to $\hat\beta_1^2 \sum_i (x_i - \bar x)^2$.

## Residual Sum of Squares (RSS / SSR / SSE)

The sum of squared errors of a fit, $\mathrm{RSS} = \sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - \hat y_i)^2$ — the quantity OLS minimizes by definition: $\hat\beta = \arg\min_\beta \|y - X\beta\|_2^2$. In matrix form $\mathrm{RSS} = y^\top (I - H) y$ where $H = X(X^\top X)^{-1} X^\top$ is the orthogonal projection onto $\mathrm{col}(X)$, so $I - H$ is the residual projector and $\mathrm{RSS} = \|M_X y\|^2$ is the squared norm of the residual vector $e = (I - H)y$. **Geometry:** $e$ is the part of $y$ orthogonal to the column space of $X$, and $(X^\top e = 0)$ are the **normal equations** that characterize the OLS solution. **Degrees of freedom and $\sigma^2$ estimation:** $(I - H)$ has rank $n - p$ (with $p$ the number of fitted parameters, including the intercept), so under the Gauss–Markov assumptions $\mathbb{E}[\mathrm{RSS}] = \sigma^2 (n - p)$, giving the unbiased noise-variance estimator $\hat\sigma^2 = \mathrm{RSS}/(n-p)$. Under Gaussian errors, $\mathrm{RSS}/\sigma^2 \sim \chi^2_{n-p}$ and is independent of $\hat\beta$. **Role across statistics:** it is the objective in $F$-tests for nested models (ratio of $\Delta \mathrm{RSS}$ to unrestricted MSE), the numerator of $R^2 = 1 - \mathrm{RSS}/\mathrm{TSS}$, the basis of AIC / BIC / $C_p$ (which penalize $\log \mathrm{RSS}$ by a complexity term), and equal (up to a constant) to the negative log-likelihood in the Gaussian case — so maximum likelihood and least squares coincide. In the bivariate case $\mathrm{RSS} = \mathrm{TSS}\,(1 - r^2)$, linking residual variance directly to the Pearson correlation.

## Pure-Error Sum of Squares

The within-group scatter in a regression design that contains *replicates* (multiple observations at the same predictor configuration). If the $N$ observations fall into $n$ distinct $x$-groups with within-group means $\bar y_g$, then
$$\mathrm{SS}_{\mathrm{PE}} \;=\; \sum_{g=1}^{n}\sum_{i \in g} (y_{gi} - \bar y_g)^2, \qquad \mathrm{df} = N - n.$$
It is *model-free*: the fitted values $\hat y_g$ do not appear, and $\mathrm{SS}_{\mathrm{PE}}$ depends only on how dispersed the responses are *within* each replicated predictor configuration. Under i.i.d. errors with variance $\sigma^2$, $\mathbb{E}[\mathrm{SS}_{\mathrm{PE}}] = (N - n)\sigma^2$, giving a **model-free unbiased estimator of the irreducible noise variance** $\hat\sigma^2_{\mathrm{PE}} = \mathrm{SS}_{\mathrm{PE}}/(N - n)$. Contrast with the usual OLS estimator $\hat\sigma^2 = \mathrm{RSS}/(N - p)$, which is only unbiased when the model is correctly specified — if the model is misspecified, $\hat\sigma^2$ absorbs approximation error, whereas $\hat\sigma^2_{\mathrm{PE}}$ does not. The decomposition $\mathrm{RSS} = \mathrm{SS}_{\mathrm{LOF}} + \mathrm{SS}_{\mathrm{PE}}$ isolates this: the pure-error piece is what would remain even if the true conditional mean $\mathbb{E}[y \mid x]$ were used in place of the fit. **In learning-theory terms,** $\mathrm{SS}_{\mathrm{PE}}/N$ is a plug-in estimate of the **Bayes risk** at the sampled $x$-values — a lower bound on any hypothesis class's achievable mean-squared error. Without replicates $\mathrm{SS}_{\mathrm{PE}} = 0$ by construction ($n = N$, $\bar y_g = y_{gi}$), and noise cannot be separated from misspecification using the data alone.

## Lack-of-Fit Sum of Squares

A further decomposition of the residual sum of squares that separates two qualitatively different sources of error whenever the design contains *replicates* — multiple observations at the same predictor configuration. Let the $N$ observations fall into $n$ distinct $x$-groups, with $\bar y_{g}$ the within-group mean and $\hat y_g$ the fitted value at the group's predictor. Then
$$\underbrace{\sum_{g}\sum_{i \in g}(y_{gi} - \hat y_g)^2}_{\mathrm{RSS}} \;=\; \underbrace{\sum_{g} n_g (\bar y_g - \hat y_g)^2}_{\mathrm{SS}_{\mathrm{LOF}}} \;+\; \underbrace{\sum_{g}\sum_{i \in g}(y_{gi} - \bar y_g)^2}_{\mathrm{SS}_{\mathrm{PE}}}.$$
The **pure-error** term $\mathrm{SS}_{\mathrm{PE}}$ captures only the irreducible within-group scatter — it depends on the noise, not on the model. The **lack-of-fit** term $\mathrm{SS}_{\mathrm{LOF}}$ captures how far the model's fitted values miss the group means. The **F-test for lack of fit** compares their mean squares:
$$F = \frac{\mathrm{SS}_{\mathrm{LOF}}/(n-p)}{\mathrm{SS}_{\mathrm{PE}}/(N-n)} \;\sim\; F_{n-p,\; N-n} \quad \text{under the null that the model form is correct},$$
with large $F$ indicating systematic misspecification beyond noise.

**Connection to realizability in statistical learning.** For a fixed hypothesis class $\mathcal{H}$ (e.g., linear functions $h_\beta(x) = x^\top \beta$) and a data distribution, the target is *realizable* iff there exists $h^* \in \mathcal{H}$ with zero population risk — i.e., $\mathbb{E}[y \mid x] \in \mathcal{H}$ with all residual variance coming from label noise. The lack-of-fit test is precisely the empirical diagnostic of this condition at observed $x$-values: $\mathrm{SS}_{\mathrm{PE}}$ estimates irreducible Bayes noise, and $\mathrm{SS}_{\mathrm{LOF}}$ estimates the *approximation error* — the gap between the conditional mean $\mathbb{E}[y \mid x]$ and the best element of $\mathcal{H}$. Rejecting $H_0$ (significant $\mathrm{LOF}$) is statistical evidence that the learning problem is *non-realizable* under $\mathcal{H}$: no linear hypothesis can attain the Bayes risk, and the approximation error (bias of the class) is nonzero. Failing to reject is consistent with (but does not prove) realizability. This is why replicates are so valuable: without them, irreducible noise and model misspecification cannot be disentangled from $\mathrm{RSS}$ alone.

## Path Analysis

An early and comparatively simple causal-inference method for *observed* variables, introduced by Sewall Wright (c. 1918, in genetics) as a way to trace how correlations arise from an assumed directed causal structure. A path model is a directed graph — usually a DAG — whose nodes are standardized observed variables and whose edges carry *path coefficients* (partial regression coefficients in standardized units). Each endogenous variable is written as a linear combination of its parents plus a disturbance, $y_i = \sum_{j \in \mathrm{pa}(i)} p_{ij} y_j + \varepsilon_i$, with the $\varepsilon_i$ uncorrelated across nodes. **Wright's tracing rules** decompose the correlation between any two variables into a sum of products of path coefficients along all admissible paths (forward-forward, backward-forward through a common cause, etc., subject to rules that rule out colliders and double-traversal). This cleanly separates a correlation into **direct effect** (the single arrow), **indirect effects** (chains through mediators), and **spurious components** (shared causes). Estimation: with all variables observed and the graph acyclic, each structural equation reduces to a multiple regression of a node on its parents, so path coefficients are just standardized OLS coefficients. **Relation to SEM:** path analysis is exactly the special case of SEM in which every construct is measured by a single observed indicator with no measurement error — SEM extends it by adding a confirmatory-factor measurement model for latent constructs. It also generalizes multiple regression (single outcome, one equation) to a *system* of regressions tied together by shared variables. **Assumptions and caveats:** linearity, additivity, no omitted common causes (no unmeasured confounders), correct arrow directions, and typically acyclicity. None of these are testable from data alone without auxiliary assumptions — path analysis gives the *consequences* of a causal hypothesis for the covariance structure, not a proof of causality. Modern graphical causal inference (Pearl's do-calculus, structural causal models) subsumes and formalizes path analysis, making the identifiability conditions explicit.

## Structural Equation Modeling (SEM)

A family of multivariate statistical techniques for testing hypothesized causal structures among *latent* (unobserved) variables, each of which is measured through multiple *observed* indicators. An SEM specifies two linked sub-models. The **measurement model** is a confirmatory factor analysis: observed indicators $x$ load on latent factors $\xi$ via $x = \Lambda \xi + \delta$, where $\Lambda$ holds the factor loadings and $\delta$ captures measurement error (with specified covariance structure $\Theta_\delta$). The **structural model** specifies directed relationships among the latents: $\eta = B\eta + \Gamma \xi + \zeta$, where $\eta$ are endogenous latents, $\xi$ exogenous, and $\zeta$ structural disturbances. Together these imply a model-implied covariance matrix $\Sigma(\theta)$ as a function of the parameters $\theta = (\Lambda, B, \Gamma, \Theta_\delta, \Phi, \Psi)$, and estimation fits $\Sigma(\theta)$ to the sample covariance $S$. **Relation to older methods:** SEM subsumes *path analysis* (which has structural equations but no latent variables / measurement error) and *confirmatory factor analysis* (which has the measurement model but no structural regression among factors) — SEM = CFA + path analysis with measurement-error correction. **Estimation:** *covariance-based* (CB-SEM) uses maximum likelihood by minimizing $\log|\Sigma(\theta)| + \mathrm{tr}(S \Sigma(\theta)^{-1}) - \log|S| - p$, or GLS / WLS variants for non-normal data; *variance-based* PLS-SEM iteratively estimates latent scores via weighted composites and is preferred for predictive modeling, small samples, and non-normal / formative indicators, at the cost of inconsistency under the strict common-factor model. **Identification:** parameters must be identifiable from the covariance structure — typically requiring $\geq 3$ indicators per factor, fixed scale for each latent (e.g., one loading set to 1), and acyclic or otherwise non-degenerate structural paths. **Fit assessment:** the $\chi^2$ test (exact fit), plus approximate-fit indices: RMSEA (root mean square error of approximation), CFI (comparative fit index), TLI, SRMR (standardized root mean square residual) — each with conventional cutoffs (e.g., RMSEA $< 0.06$, CFI $> 0.95$) that are heuristics rather than proofs of validity. **Uses:** psychology (measuring constructs like anxiety or cognitive ability), sociology (testing theories of social mobility), marketing (customer satisfaction / brand equity models), epidemiology, and econometrics — anywhere the substantive theory posits latent constructs related causally through an observable indicator system.

## Matrix / Tensor Deflation

An iterative technique for extracting structure (eigenpairs, singular triplets, low-rank components) one factor at a time by subtracting off the contribution just found and recursing on the residual. **Matrix case:** after computing a dominant eigenpair $(\lambda_1, v_1)$ of a symmetric matrix $A$, form the deflated matrix $A^{(1)} = A - \lambda_1 v_1 v_1^\top$, which has the same remaining spectrum as $A$ but with $\lambda_1$ replaced by $0$ — so running power iteration on $A^{(1)}$ recovers $\lambda_2$. The SVD analogue deflates by $A^{(1)} = A - \sigma_1 u_1 v_1^\top$. More generally, *Hotelling deflation* uses $A - \lambda_1 v_1 v_1^\top$, *Wielandt deflation* uses $A - v_1 w_1^\top$ for some $w_1$ chosen so the new matrix has the desired eigenvalue at zero, and *orthogonal (projection) deflation* uses $(I - v_1 v_1^\top) A (I - v_1 v_1^\top)$. In **NIPALS / PLS**, each iteration deflates both $X$ and $y$ by the rank-1 contribution of the extracted score/loading pair — this is what makes successive components orthogonal. In sparse dictionary learning and matching pursuit, deflation of the residual signal after selecting each atom plays the same role. **Tensor case:** for a higher-order tensor $\mathcal{T} \in \mathbb{R}^{n_1 \times \dots \times n_d}$, deflation subtracts a rank-1 term $\sigma\, u^{(1)} \circ u^{(2)} \circ \dots \circ u^{(d)}$ (outer product of vectors, one per mode) to approximate the CP / canonical polyadic decomposition greedily. Unlike the matrix case, **tensor deflation is not in general optimal**: the best rank-$r$ CP approximation is *not* obtained by successively peeling off best rank-1 approximations (this is Kolda's observation; the set of low-rank tensors is not closed, and greedy rank-1 extraction can even diverge in cost). Remedies include simultaneous block updates (ALS on all components jointly), orthogonal constraints (higher-order SVD / Tucker deflation), and deflation-with-correction schemes. **Numerical caveats:** deflation accumulates roundoff — after several steps the residual may be polluted by lost orthogonality, so practical eigensolvers (Lanczos, ARPACK) prefer explicit re-orthogonalization or restart strategies over naive Hotelling deflation.

## Cauchy Interlacing Theorem (Poincaré Separation Theorem)

An eigenvalue interlacing result for compressions of a Hermitian matrix. Let $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$ (or $\mathbb{C}^{n \times n}$) be Hermitian with eigenvalues $\lambda_1 \geq \lambda_2 \geq \dots \geq \lambda_n$, and let $B \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times r}$ be semi-orthogonal ($B^\top B = I_r$, so $r \leq n$). Then the $r$ eigenvalues $\mu_1 \geq \dots \geq \mu_r$ of the compressed matrix $B^\top A B$ satisfy
$$\lambda_i \;\geq\; \mu_i \;\geq\; \lambda_{n-r+i}, \qquad i = 1, \dots, r.$$
Each $\mu_i$ is squeezed between the $i$-th eigenvalue of $A$ from above and the $(n-r+i)$-th from below — the compressed spectrum sits inside the original one, "interlaced" at both ends. **Geometric picture:** $B^\top A B$ is the matrix of the quadratic form $x \mapsto x^\top A x$ restricted to the $r$-dimensional subspace $\mathrm{range}(B)$, so the theorem bounds the extremal values of the Rayleigh quotient of $A$ on that subspace. **Principal-submatrix case ($r = n-1$):** when $B$ selects a coordinate subspace (drops one row and the matching column of $A$), one gets the classical Cauchy interlacing $\lambda_i \geq \mu_i \geq \lambda_{i+1}$ — the spectra of $A$ and any principal $(n-1)\times(n-1)$ submatrix strictly alternate. Iterating gives interlacing for any principal submatrix. **Proof sketch:** a direct consequence of the min–max theorem: $\mu_i = \max_{\dim M = i, M \subseteq \mathrm{range}(B)} \min_{x \in M, \|x\|=1} x^\top A x$, and restricting the subspace $M$ to lie in $\mathrm{range}(B)$ can only lower the max-min (giving $\mu_i \leq \lambda_i$); the other bound comes from the dual max-min characterization. **Applications:** predicting how mechanical mode frequencies shift when a constraint is added (the constrained system's frequencies interlace the unconstrained ones), bounding singular values of submatrices, and spectral perturbation analysis in numerical linear algebra (e.g., convergence of Lanczos and Ritz values, which are exactly the eigenvalues of $B^\top A B$ for an orthonormal Krylov basis $B$).

## Semi-orthogonal Matrix

A real rectangular matrix $Q \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ (with $m \neq n$) whose columns *or* rows are orthonormal — equivalently, $Q^\top Q = I_n$ when $m \geq n$ (orthonormal columns, the "tall" case) or $Q Q^\top = I_m$ when $m \leq n$ (orthonormal rows, the "wide" case). Only one of $Q^\top Q$ and $Q Q^\top$ can equal the identity in the non-square case; the other is a projection onto the column or row space. Equivalently, $Q$ is semi-orthogonal iff it has full rank and all its non-zero singular values equal $1$. **As an isometry:** a tall $Q$ with $Q^\top Q = I_n$ gives $\|Qx\|_2 = \|x\|_2$ for all $x \in \mathbb{R}^n$, so it embeds $\mathbb{R}^n$ isometrically into $\mathbb{R}^m$ — this is the property used in the orthonormal-transform step of the MNLS proof via SVD. **Relation to orthogonal / unitary:** a square semi-orthogonal matrix is exactly an orthogonal matrix ($Q^\top Q = QQ^\top = I$); the complex analogue (with $Q^* Q = I$ or $QQ^* = I$) is a *semi-unitary* matrix, sometimes called an isometry or co-isometry. **Appearance in SVD:** in the thin SVD $A = U\Sigma V^\top$ with $A \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ of rank $k$, $U \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times k}$ and $V \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times k}$ are both semi-orthogonal (tall, with orthonormal columns). The full SVD instead uses square orthogonal $U$ and $V$ padded with a null-space basis.

## Min-Max Theorem (Courant–Fischer–Weyl)

A variational characterization of the eigenvalues of a Hermitian (or self-adjoint) operator as extrema of the Rayleigh quotient $R_A(x) = \langle x, Ax \rangle / \langle x, x \rangle$. For a Hermitian matrix $A$ with eigenvalues ordered $\lambda_1 \geq \lambda_2 \geq \dots \geq \lambda_n$,
$$\lambda_k = \max_{\dim M = k} \; \min_{\substack{x \in M \\ \|x\|=1}} \langle x, Ax\rangle = \min_{\dim M = n-k+1} \; \max_{\substack{x \in M \\ \|x\|=1}} \langle x, Ax\rangle,$$
with the maxima (resp. minima) taken over subspaces $M \subseteq \mathbb{C}^n$ of the stated dimension. The extremizers are spanned by the top-$k$ (resp. bottom-$(n-k+1)$) eigenvectors. Setting $k=1$ recovers the familiar $\lambda_1 = \max_{\|x\|=1}\langle x, Ax\rangle$; $k=n$ gives $\lambda_n = \min_{\|x\|=1} \langle x, Ax\rangle$. **Extensions:** the statement extends verbatim to compact self-adjoint operators on a Hilbert space (applied to the discrete spectrum above the essential spectrum), and to singular values via $\sigma_k(A) = \lambda_k(\sqrt{A^*A})$ — giving $\sigma_k = \max_{\dim M = k} \min_{x \in M, \|x\|=1} \|Ax\|$. **Consequences:** (i) **Cauchy interlacing** — eigenvalues of a principal submatrix interlace those of the full matrix; (ii) **Weyl's inequalities** — for Hermitian $A, B$, $\lambda_{i+j-1}(A+B) \leq \lambda_i(A) + \lambda_j(B)$, in particular $|\lambda_k(A+E) - \lambda_k(A)| \leq \|E\|_{\mathrm{op}}$, so Hermitian spectra are stable under perturbation; (iii) **monotonicity in the Loewner order** — $A \succeq B \implies \lambda_k(A) \geq \lambda_k(B)$ for all $k$. The theorem fails without self-adjointness: for a non-normal $A$ the Rayleigh quotient's range (the numerical range) generally strictly contains the spectrum — a nilpotent $\begin{pmatrix}0 & 1 \\ 0 & 0\end{pmatrix}$ has only $0$ as an eigenvalue but numerical range a disk of radius $1/2$.

## Fat Shattering Dimension

A scale-sensitive complexity measure for classes of real-valued functions, generalizing VC dimension. A set $S = \{x_1, \dots, x_n\}$ is $\gamma$-shattered by a function class $\mathcal{G}$ if there exists a witness vector $r \in \mathbb{R}^n$ such that for every labeling $y \in \{-1, +1\}^n$, some $g \in \mathcal{G}$ satisfies $y_i(g(x_i) - r_i) \geq \gamma$ — meaning $g$ can be pushed at least $\gamma$ above or below each threshold $r_i$ in whichever direction the labeling demands. The fat shattering dimension $\text{fat}_\gamma(\mathcal{G})$ is the size of the largest set that can be $\gamma$-shattered. Unlike VC dimension (which is the $\gamma = 0$ binary case), it captures the margin structure of real-valued function classes and is central to generalization bounds for regression and margin-based classification.

## Resolution of the Identity

The statement that the identity operator on a Hilbert space can be decomposed as a sum (or integral) over a complete set of projectors. In the discrete case, given an orthonormal basis $\{|e_i\rangle\}$, this is $I = \sum_i |e_i\rangle\langle e_i|$ — any vector $|v\rangle = \sum_i \langle e_i | v \rangle |e_i\rangle$ is recovered by "inserting the identity." For a self-adjoint operator $T$ with continuous spectrum, the sum generalizes to a projection-valued measure $\Omega_T$: $I = \int_{-\infty}^{\infty} d\Omega_T(\lambda)$, and the spectral theorem gives $T = \int \lambda\, d\Omega_T(\lambda)$. In physics (Dirac notation) the continuous case is written formally as $I = \int |x\rangle\langle x|\,dx$, which is how one inserts a completeness relation to move between position, momentum, or energy representations. The Borel functional calculus extends this further: given the spectral decomposition, any Borel function $h$ can be applied to $T$ via $h(T) = \int h(\lambda)\,d\Omega_T(\lambda)$.

## Woodbury Matrix Identity

For $A \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times n}$, $U \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times k}$, $C \in \mathbb{R}^{k \times k}$, $V \in \mathbb{R}^{k \times n}$ with $A$ and $C$ invertible: $(A + UCV)^{-1} = A^{-1} - A^{-1}U(C^{-1} + VA^{-1}U)^{-1}VA^{-1}$. The key insight is that inverting the $n \times n$ matrix $A + UCV$ reduces to inverting the $k \times k$ matrix $C^{-1} + VA^{-1}U$, so when $k \ll n$ and $A^{-1}$ is already known, the update is much cheaper than a full $O(n^3)$ inversion. When $U = u$ and $V = v^\top$ are vectors (rank-1 case), this reduces to the Sherman–Morrison formula. Derived from block inversion of a $(n+k) \times (n+k)$ matrix via the Schur complement. Central to the Kalman filter (where the state covariance is updated by a low-rank observation model), recursive least squares, and the matrix determinant lemma $\det(A + UCV) = \det(C^{-1} + VA^{-1}U)\,\det(C)\,\det(A)$.

## Sherman–Morrison Formula

Given an invertible matrix $A$ and column vectors $u, v$, the inverse of the rank-1 update $A + uv^\top$ is $(A + uv^\top)^{-1} = A^{-1} - \frac{A^{-1} u v^\top A^{-1}}{1 + v^\top A^{-1} u}$, valid whenever $1 + v^\top A^{-1} u \neq 0$. Instead of recomputing the full inverse from scratch ($O(n^3)$), this updates it in $O(n^2)$ using only matrix-vector products with the already-known $A^{-1}$. A special case of the Woodbury identity $(A + UCV)^{-1} = A^{-1} - A^{-1}U(C^{-1} + VA^{-1}U)^{-1}VA^{-1}$, which handles rank-$k$ updates. Used in quasi-Newton methods (e.g. BFGS, where each step applies a rank-2 update to the approximate inverse Hessian) and online/streaming settings where a matrix is modified one row or column at a time.

## Determinant–Trace Identity: $\det(e^A) = e^{\mathrm{tr}(A)}$

For any square matrix $A \in \mathbb{C}^{n \times n}$,

$$\det\!\bigl(e^A\bigr) \;=\; e^{\mathrm{tr}(A)}.$$

**Intuition.** This is the matrix lift of the scalar identity $\exp\bigl(\sum_i \lambda_i\bigr) = \prod_i \exp(\lambda_i)$. Three spectral facts do all the work: **(1) the eigenvalues of $e^A$ are $e^{\lambda_i}$** where $\lambda_i$ are the eigenvalues of $A$ (since every analytic function of $A$ acts as the corresponding scalar function on each eigenvalue — if $Av = \lambda v$ then $e^A v = e^\lambda v$, and this extends to the generalized eigenspaces in the Jordan form); **(2) $\mathrm{tr}(A) = \sum_i \lambda_i$**; **(3) $\det(M) = \prod_i \mu_i$** for $M$'s eigenvalues. Chaining them: $\det(e^A) = \prod_i e^{\lambda_i} = e^{\sum_i \lambda_i} = e^{\mathrm{tr}(A)}$. So the identity is just "$\exp$ converts sums to products" read through the spectral decomposition.

**One-line proof via Schur / Jordan.** Triangularize $A = T^{-1}\Lambda T$ with $\Lambda$ upper-triangular carrying the eigenvalues $\lambda_1, \ldots, \lambda_n$ on its diagonal (counted with algebraic multiplicity). Then $e^A = T^{-1} e^\Lambda T$ is also triangular with diagonal entries $e^{\lambda_i}$, so
$$\det(e^A) = \prod_i e^{\lambda_i} = e^{\sum_i \lambda_i} = e^{\mathrm{tr}(A)}.$$
Holds over $\mathbb{R}$ or $\mathbb{C}$, for any $A$ (no diagonalizability required — the Jordan form has the same diagonal).

**Differential-equations proof (Liouville / Jacobi).** The matrix ODE $\Phi'(t) = A\Phi(t)$, $\Phi(0) = I$ has solution $\Phi(t) = e^{tA}$. Its determinant satisfies Jacobi's formula $\tfrac{d}{dt}\det\Phi = \mathrm{tr}(A)\det\Phi$, giving $\det\Phi(t) = e^{t\,\mathrm{tr}(A)}$; set $t = 1$.

**Why it matters.** (i) The map $\exp : \mathfrak{gl}(n) \to \mathrm{GL}(n)$ sends the trace-zero subalgebra $\mathfrak{sl}(n)$ onto (a neighborhood of) the unit-determinant group $\mathrm{SL}(n)$ — this is the Lie-algebra / Lie-group link for the volume-preserving flows. (ii) In continuous dynamical systems $\dot x = Ax$, the phase-volume expansion rate is $\mathrm{tr}(A)$ — a system is **volume-preserving** iff $\mathrm{tr}(A) = 0$, **dissipative** iff $\mathrm{tr}(A) < 0$ (Liouville's theorem for Hamiltonian flows is the nonlinear generalization). (iii) In numerical linear algebra this gives the cheapest stable way to evaluate $\log\det M$ for $M \succ 0$: $\log\det M = \mathrm{tr}(\log M)$, which plays nicely with Lanczos / Hutchinson stochastic trace estimation without forming eigenvalues. (iv) For probabilistic models (Gaussians, normalizing flows), the Jacobian log-determinant of an infinitesimal transformation $I + \epsilon A$ is $\epsilon\,\mathrm{tr}(A) + O(\epsilon^2)$ — the identity's linearization.

## Whitening Transformation

A linear change of variables that turns a random vector $X \in \mathbb{R}^n$ with mean $\mu$ and covariance $\Sigma$ (assumed PD) into a vector $Y = W(X - \mu)$ with $\mathbb{E}[Y] = 0$ and $\mathrm{Cov}(Y) = I$ — so that $Y$'s components are **uncorrelated with unit variance**, like white noise (hence "whitening" / "sphering"). The defining condition on the whitening matrix is
$$W \Sigma W^\top = I \quad \Longleftrightarrow \quad W^\top W = \Sigma^{-1}.$$
This has infinitely many solutions — if $W$ whitens then so does $QW$ for any orthogonal $Q$ — so one picks a specific $W$ per application. **Common choices.** (i) *ZCA / Mahalanobis whitening:* $W = \Sigma^{-1/2}$ (the symmetric PD square root). Uniquely minimizes $\mathbb{E}\|Y - X\|^2$ among whitenings — preserves the original features as much as possible. (ii) *PCA whitening:* $W = \Lambda^{-1/2} U^\top$ where $\Sigma = U\Lambda U^\top$. Axis-aligns the decorrelated data to the principal components, useful when you want to rank dimensions by variance or truncate. (iii) *Cholesky whitening:* $W = L^{-1}$ with $\Sigma = LL^\top$. Triangular and cheap — the default in GLS-style implementations since it corresponds to regressing $i$-th observation on earlier ones. (iv) *ZCA-cor / PCA-cor:* variants whitening the correlation matrix first, which preserves interpretability of features with heterogeneous units. The non-uniqueness is a feature: ZCA minimizes distortion, PCA aligns with variance, Cholesky respects causal/temporal ordering. **Uses.** In **GLS**, premultiplying the linear model by $L^{-1}$ (where $\Omega = LL^\top$) whitens the noise and reduces to plain OLS on the transformed system — which is exactly the Gauss–Markov/Aitken derivation. In **ML preprocessing**, whitening inputs removes the feature covariance so downstream algorithms (ICA, k-means, many clustering and matching-pursuit methods) see an isotropic geometry; PCA whitening followed by rotation is the standard first step in **ICA** (decorrelation is necessary but not sufficient for independence). In **neural nets**, batch-norm is a stochastic running-mean standardization and ZCA-whitening augmentation has been used as data preprocessing; "whitened" features behave better for methods sensitive to condition number. **Caveats.** In high dimensions $\hat\Sigma$ from finite samples is ill-conditioned or singular, so practical whitening uses shrinkage-regularized covariance $(\hat\Sigma + \lambda I)^{-1/2}$ (Ledoit–Wolf). In infinite-dimensional / functional settings the covariance operator has unbounded inverse, and only *partial* whitening is meaningful.

## Gauss–Markov Theorem

The foundational optimality result for ordinary least squares (OLS). For the linear model $y = X\beta + \varepsilon$ under four assumptions —
1. **Linearity in parameters:** $\mathbb{E}[y \mid X] = X\beta$ (the regressors may be nonlinear in raw variables).
2. **Strict exogeneity:** $\mathbb{E}[\varepsilon \mid X] = 0$ (errors uncorrelated with the regressors in expectation).
3. **Full column rank:** $X$ has rank $p$, so $X^\top X$ is invertible.
4. **Spherical errors:** $\mathrm{Cov}(\varepsilon \mid X) = \sigma^2 I$ — homoskedastic *and* uncorrelated across observations.

— the OLS estimator $\hat\beta = (X^\top X)^{-1} X^\top y$ is **BLUE**: among all **linear unbiased** estimators $\tilde\beta = Cy$ (with $C$ a function of $X$ only and $\mathbb{E}[\tilde\beta] = \beta$ for all $\beta$), OLS has minimum variance in the Loewner-order sense,
$$\mathrm{Cov}(\tilde\beta) \;-\; \mathrm{Cov}(\hat\beta) \;=\; \mathrm{Cov}(\tilde\beta) - \sigma^2(X^\top X)^{-1} \;\succeq\; 0.$$
Equivalently, for every linear combination $c^\top\beta$, $\mathrm{Var}(c^\top \hat\beta) \leq \mathrm{Var}(c^\top \tilde\beta)$. **What it does *not* require.** No Gaussianity, no i.i.d.-ness beyond the second-moment conditions, no symmetry or finite higher moments. Under Gaussian noise OLS becomes the MLE and additionally attains the Cramér–Rao bound among *all* unbiased estimators (linear or not), but Gauss–Markov itself restricts attention to linear estimators. **What it does not say.** Nothing about bias-variance tradeoff: shrinkage estimators (ridge, James–Stein, PCR) are *biased* and therefore outside the theorem's scope — and indeed achieve lower MSE than OLS in many regimes (see the Shrinkage entry). **Aitken's extension.** When assumption 4 fails — $\mathrm{Cov}(\varepsilon) = \Omega$ for some known non-scalar PD matrix $\Omega$ — OLS remains unbiased but loses BLUE status; **GLS** with the whitened objective $\|y - X\beta\|_{\Omega^{-1}}^2$ recovers it (Aitken 1935). The heteroskedastic-only special case recovers **WLS** optimality with weights $1/\sigma_i^2$. **Proof idea.** For any competing $\tilde\beta = Cy$ with $CX = I$ (the unbiasedness constraint), write $C = (X^\top X)^{-1}X^\top + D$ with $DX = 0$, expand $\mathrm{Cov}(\tilde\beta) = \sigma^2 CC^\top = \sigma^2(X^\top X)^{-1} + \sigma^2 DD^\top$, and observe $DD^\top \succeq 0$ with equality iff $D = 0$.

## Weighted Least Squares (WLS)

An OLS variant for the linear model $y = X\beta + \varepsilon$ with *heteroskedastic* but *uncorrelated* errors: $\mathrm{Var}(\varepsilon_i) = \sigma_i^2$, $\mathrm{Cov}(\varepsilon_i, \varepsilon_j) = 0$ for $i \neq j$. The estimator minimizes a weighted sum of squared residuals,
$$\hat\beta_{\mathrm{WLS}} = \arg\min_\beta \sum_{i=1}^n w_i (y_i - x_i^\top \beta)^2 = (X^\top W X)^{-1} X^\top W y,$$
where $W = \mathrm{diag}(w_1, \dots, w_n)$ is the diagonal weight matrix with **BLUE weights** $w_i = 1/\sigma_i^2$ — down-weighting noisier observations exactly in proportion to their precision. **Interpretation:** equivalent to OLS on the rescaled problem $\tilde y_i = y_i/\sigma_i$, $\tilde x_i = x_i/\sigma_i$, which has homoskedastic unit-variance noise. Under independent Gaussian errors $\varepsilon_i \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \sigma_i^2)$ with *known* $\sigma_i$, WLS is the maximum-likelihood estimator; its covariance is $\mathrm{Cov}(\hat\beta_{\mathrm{WLS}}) = (X^\top W X)^{-1}$ (when $w_i = 1/\sigma_i^2$). **Relationship to GLS:** WLS is exactly GLS with a diagonal noise covariance $\Omega = W^{-1} = \mathrm{diag}(\sigma_i^2)$ — no cross-observation correlation is modeled. **Feasible WLS:** when $\sigma_i^2$ is unknown, fit a preliminary OLS, estimate a variance function $\sigma_i^2 = g(x_i; \theta)$ from the squared residuals, and plug in $\hat w_i = 1/\hat\sigma_i^2$; iterating gives *iteratively reweighted least squares* (IRLS), which is also the engine behind M-estimators (robust regression) and GLM fitting.

## Half-Quadratic Regularizer

A framework for minimizing objectives containing a *non-quadratic* penalty $\phi(r)$ — on residuals, gradients, or coefficients — by replacing $\phi$ with a family of **quadratic surrogates** parameterized by auxiliary weights, then alternating between updating the weights in closed form and solving a weighted quadratic subproblem for the model parameters. Under mild regularity, any sufficiently well-behaved $\phi$ admits the **multiplicative half-quadratic decomposition** (Geman–Reynolds 1992)
$$\phi(r) \;=\; \min_{w \geq 0} \left\{ \tfrac{1}{2}\, w\, r^2 + \psi(w) \right\},$$
where $\psi$ is a convex conjugate-like function determined by $\phi$; a dual **additive** form $\phi(r) = \min_v \{\tfrac{1}{2}(r - v)^2 + \xi(v)\}$ (Geman–Yang 1995) gives an alternative surrogate based on a shifted quadratic. Substituting into an objective of the form $F(\beta) = \sum_i \phi(y_i - x_i^\top\beta) + \text{(data term)}$ yields a joint problem in $(\beta, w)$; block-coordinate descent alternates
(i) **weight update** $w_i \gets \arg\min_w \{\tfrac12 w r_i^2 + \psi(w)\}$ (closed form, depends only on current residuals), and
(ii) **parameter update** $\beta \gets \arg\min_\beta \sum_i w_i (y_i - x_i^\top \beta)^2$ — a **weighted least-squares** solve. This is an instance of majorization–minimization (Hunter–Lange 2004): each iteration majorizes $F$ by a quadratic upper bound tight at the current iterate, so $F$ decreases monotonically, even when $F$ itself is nonconvex.

**IRLS as a special case.** Iteratively Reweighted Least Squares (see [IRLS — Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iteratively_reweighted_least_squares)) is exactly the HQ scheme applied to $\ell_p$-like penalties $\phi(r) = |r|^p$: the weight update gives
$$\beta^{(t+1)} = \arg\min_\beta \sum_i w_i^{(t)} (y_i - x_i^\top \beta)^2, \qquad w_i^{(t)} = |y_i - x_i^\top \beta^{(t)}|^{\,p-2}.$$
For $p = 2$ it collapses to a single WLS solve; for $p = 1$ (least absolute deviations, basis pursuit / compressed sensing) stabilization $w_i = 1/\max(\delta, |r_i|)$ is required, which is equivalent to the quadratic smoothing of a Huber penalty near the origin. For $0 < p < 1$ (nonconvex sparse recovery) the same recipe still converges monotonically to a stationary point.

**Scope.** HQ / IRLS handles any penalty admitting a quadratic majorant — Huber, Tukey biweight, Welsch, Geman–McClure, Cauchy, log-sum (reweighted-$\ell_1$), Charbonnier, and total variation — and is the standard engine behind robust M-estimators, Fisher scoring for GLMs, and iteratively reweighted-$\ell_1$ for enhanced sparsity (Candès–Wakin–Boyd 2008). The appeal is that each inner solve is a *convex* weighted quadratic — cheap, well-conditioned, and compatible with sparse linear algebra — even when the overall objective is non-smooth or nonconvex.

## Generalized Least Squares (GLS)

An extension of ordinary least squares for the linear model $y = X\beta + \varepsilon$ when the noise covariance is $\mathrm{Cov}(\varepsilon) = \Omega$, a known symmetric positive-definite matrix — not necessarily $\sigma^2 I$. The estimator minimizes the Mahalanobis residual $\|y - X\beta\|_{\Omega^{-1}}^2 = (y - X\beta)^\top \Omega^{-1}(y - X\beta)$, giving
$$\hat\beta_{\mathrm{GLS}} = (X^\top \Omega^{-1} X)^{-1} X^\top \Omega^{-1} y, \qquad \mathrm{Cov}(\hat\beta_{\mathrm{GLS}}) = (X^\top \Omega^{-1} X)^{-1}.$$
Operationally, GLS is OLS on a "whitened" problem: take any factor $\Omega = L L^\top$ (e.g., Cholesky) and pre-multiply by $L^{-1}$ to get $L^{-1} y = L^{-1} X \beta + L^{-1}\varepsilon$ whose noise is isotropic, then apply OLS. **Aitken's theorem (1935)** is the Gauss–Markov analogue: among linear unbiased estimators of $\beta$, $\hat\beta_{\mathrm{GLS}}$ has minimum variance (BLUE) when $\Omega$ is correctly specified. **Special cases:** $\Omega = \sigma^2 I$ recovers OLS; $\Omega$ diagonal recovers *weighted* least squares (WLS), with weights $w_i = 1/\Omega_{ii}$ that down-weight noisier observations. **Feasible GLS (FGLS):** when $\Omega$ is unknown, estimate it (typically from OLS residuals under a parametric model — AR(1) for autocorrelation, a variance function for heteroskedasticity), then plug in $\hat\Omega$; asymptotically equivalent to GLS but only approximately BLUE in finite samples. **Uses:** heteroskedastic errors (cross-sectional data with varying noise scales), autocorrelated errors (time series, panel data), clustered/grouped data, and any setting where the residuals' covariance structure is not i.i.d.

## Total Least Squares (TLS)

A regression method for the errors-in-variables setting, where both the design matrix $X$ *and* the response $y$ are noisy: $X = X_0 + E$, $y = y_0 + r$ with $X_0 \beta = y_0$. Where OLS minimizes only the vertical residual $\|y - X\beta\|^2$ (treating $X$ as exact), TLS minimizes the combined perturbation $\|[E \; r]\|_F^2$ subject to $(X - E)\beta = y - r$ — i.e., the smallest joint correction to both $X$ and $y$ that makes the system consistent. **SVD solution:** form the augmented matrix $[X \; y] \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times (p+1)}$, compute its SVD $[X\;y] = U\Sigma V^\top$, and use the right singular vector $v_{p+1}$ corresponding to the smallest singular value $\sigma_{p+1}$; partitioning $v_{p+1} = \begin{bmatrix} v_X \\ v_y \end{bmatrix}$ gives $\hat\beta_{\mathrm{TLS}} = -v_X / v_y$ (valid when $v_y \neq 0$). This is a direct consequence of the Eckart–Young theorem: zeroing out $\sigma_{p+1}$ yields the nearest rank-$p$ matrix to $[X \; y]$ in Frobenius norm, which is exactly the smallest joint perturbation making the augmented system rank-deficient (hence consistent). **Geometric interpretation:** in the univariate case (fitting a line to $(x_i, y_i)$ points), TLS minimizes *orthogonal* (perpendicular) distances to the line rather than vertical distances — this is **orthogonal regression**, and when the $x$- and $y$-errors share units and variance it coincides with Deming regression. **Comparison with OLS:** OLS is biased when $X$ is noisy (attenuation bias, pulling slopes toward zero); TLS removes that bias when the noise is isotropic across $[E \; r]$. **Extensions:** *generalized* TLS scales the augmentation by a known noise covariance; *truncated* TLS handles near-rank-deficient $[X \; y]$ by zeroing multiple trailing singular values, combining errors-in-variables correction with SVD-filter-style regularization.

## Shrinkage (Statistics)

The practice of pulling a raw estimate toward a reference value (often zero, or a grand mean) to trade bias for reduced variance and achieve lower mean squared error. The foundational result is Stein's paradox (1956) and the James–Stein estimator (1961): for estimating the mean of a multivariate Gaussian in dimension $p \geq 3$, the MLE $\hat\mu = x$ is *inadmissible* — the shrunken estimator $\hat\mu_{JS} = \left(1 - \frac{(p-2)\sigma^2}{\|x\|^2}\right) x$ strictly dominates it in MSE for every true $\mu$, despite being biased. This showed that unbiasedness is not optimality. Most modern regularization methods are shrinkage estimators: ridge regression shrinks OLS coefficients smoothly via $\hat\beta_\lambda = (X^\top X + \lambda I)^{-1} X^\top y$; lasso shrinks via soft-thresholding and induces sparsity; principal component regression shrinks by hard-thresholding in the SVD basis. Bayesian posterior means are shrinkage estimators where the prior sets the target and strength. Empirical Bayes methods estimate the shrinkage strength from the data itself (Efron–Morris). The unifying theme: combining a noisy direct estimate with "other information" — whether a prior, a low-dimensional subspace, or simply the origin — can beat using the direct estimate alone.

## Ridge, Lasso, and Elastic Net

A family of penalized least-squares estimators that differ only in the penalty norm, and together cover the most common regularized regression setups:

$$\hat\beta(\lambda, \alpha) \;=\; \arg\min_\beta \;\tfrac{1}{2}\|y - X\beta\|_2^2 \;+\; \lambda\!\left(\alpha \|\beta\|_1 + \tfrac{1-\alpha}{2}\|\beta\|_2^2\right).$$

$\alpha = 0$ is **ridge** (pure $\ell_2$), $\alpha = 1$ is **lasso** (pure $\ell_1$), $0 < \alpha < 1$ is **elastic net**.

**Ridge ($\ell_2$ / Tikhonov).** Closed form $\hat\beta_{\mathrm{ridge}} = (X^\top X + \lambda I)^{-1} X^\top y$. Replaces the possibly ill-conditioned Gram matrix with the strictly positive-definite $X^\top X + \lambda I$, so a unique solution exists even when $p > n$ or $X$ is rank-deficient. **SVD picture:** with $X = U\Sigma V^\top$, ridge is the smooth spectral filter $\hat\beta = \sum_i \frac{\sigma_i}{\sigma_i^2 + \lambda}(u_i^\top y)\,v_i$ — damp small-$\sigma_i$ directions (high-variance in OLS), pass through large-$\sigma_i$ directions almost unchanged. Contrast with PCR's hard-threshold filter. **Bayesian interpretation:** MAP under a Gaussian prior $\beta \sim \mathcal{N}(0, \tau^2 I)$ with $\lambda = \sigma^2/\tau^2$. **Effective degrees of freedom:** $\mathrm{tr}[H_\lambda] = \sum_i \sigma_i^2/(\sigma_i^2 + \lambda)$, used in GCV / AIC tuning. Shrinks every coefficient continuously toward zero but sets none exactly to zero — **no sparsity**. Same machinery appears as **Tikhonov regularization** for inverse problems and **weight decay** in neural networks.

**Lasso ($\ell_1$).** Tibshirani (1996). The $\ell_1$ penalty is the tightest convex relaxation of the non-convex $\ell_0$ count penalty, and — unlike $\ell_2$ — produces **exact sparsity**: coefficients are set to exactly zero, so lasso performs simultaneous estimation and variable selection. **Geometry:** the $\ell_1$ ball has corners on the coordinate axes, so level sets of the least-squares objective typically touch it at a corner, forcing some $\hat\beta_j = 0$. **Orthonormal design closed form:** when $X^\top X = I$, lasso is coordinatewise **soft-thresholding** $\hat\beta_j = \mathrm{sign}(z_j)\max(|z_j| - \lambda, 0)$ with $z = X^\top y$ — compare ridge's proportional shrinkage $z_j/(1+\lambda)$ and best-subset's hard thresholding $z_j\,\mathbb{1}[|z_j| > \lambda]$. **KKT conditions** characterize the active set: on the support, $X_S^\top(y - X\hat\beta) = \lambda\,\mathrm{sign}(\hat\beta_S)$; off-support, $|x_j^\top(y - X\hat\beta)| \leq \lambda$. **Bayesian interpretation:** MAP under a Laplace prior. **Solution path:** piecewise linear in $\lambda$ (LARS; Efron et al., 2004). **Guarantees:** under restricted-eigenvalue or irrepresentability conditions on $X$, lasso achieves near-minimax prediction error $O(\sigma^2 s \log p / n)$ and consistent support recovery, where $s$ is the sparsity of the true $\beta$. **Caveats:** biased on the selected support (selected coefficients are shrunk even when truly nonzero); unstable under high predictor correlation (arbitrarily picks one of a correlated group and drops the rest); can select at most $n$ variables in the $p > n$ regime. These two failure modes motivate elastic net.

**Elastic Net.** Zou & Hastie (2005). A convex combination of the two penalties. At $\alpha = 1$ it is lasso; at $\alpha = 0$ it is ridge. The $\ell_2$ term makes the effective Gram matrix $X^\top X + \lambda(1-\alpha) I$ well-conditioned (fixing lasso's instability under correlation) while the $\ell_1$ term preserves sparsity. **Grouping effect:** coefficients of two predictors with correlation $\rho$ differ by $O(\sqrt{1 - \rho})$, so tightly correlated predictors enter or leave the model together instead of being arbitrarily picked between. In the $p > n$ regime, elastic net can select more than $n$ variables, unlike lasso. **Equivalence:** elastic net on $(X, y)$ rewrites as lasso on an augmented design $\tilde X = \binom{X}{\sqrt{\lambda(1-\alpha)}\,I}$, $\tilde y = \binom{y}{0}$, so any lasso solver handles it. **Tuning:** usually $\alpha$ on a coarse grid (e.g. 0.1, 0.5, 0.9, 1.0) × $\lambda$ on a fine regularization path, chosen by cross-validation. `glmnet`'s parametrization matches the formula above exactly.

**When to prefer which.** Ridge: many small effects, no reason to expect sparsity, ill-conditioning is the main problem. Lasso: a truly sparse underlying signal with uncorrelated or well-conditioned predictors. Elastic net: sparse-ish signal with correlated predictors, or $p \gg n$ where lasso's $n$-variable cap bites.

**Unified spectral / filter view.** All three are SVD filters: ridge filter $\sigma_i/(\sigma_i^2 + \lambda)$ (smooth), PCR filter $\mathbb{1}[i \leq k]/\sigma_i$ (hard truncation), lasso / elastic net filter is coordinate-system-dependent and does not admit a simple $\sigma_i$-only expression — the $\ell_1$ penalty couples the SVD basis to the standard basis, which is exactly what makes it select variables in the original coordinates rather than in principal directions.

## Principal Component Regression (PCR)

A regression method that performs OLS after projecting the (centered) design matrix $X \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times p}$ onto its top $k$ principal components. Given the SVD $X = U\Sigma V^\top$, form derived covariates $W_k = X V_k$ using the top-$k$ right singular vectors, solve $\hat\gamma_k = \arg\min_\gamma \|y - W_k \gamma\|^2$ by OLS, and map back: $\hat\beta_k = V_k \hat\gamma_k = \sum_{i=1}^{k} \frac{u_i^\top y}{\sigma_i} v_i$. When $k = p$ (and $X$ has full column rank), PCR reduces to OLS. For $k < p$ it is a shrinkage estimator: variance strictly decreases, $\text{Var}(\hat\beta_{\mathrm{ols}}) - \text{Var}(\hat\beta_k) \succeq 0$, at the cost of bias unless the true $\beta$ lies in $\text{span}(V_k)$. **Relation to SVD filtering / ridge:** PCR corresponds to the hard-threshold filter $g(\sigma_i) = \sigma_i^{-1} \cdot \mathbb{1}[i \leq k]$, whereas ridge uses the smooth filter $g(\sigma_i) = \sigma_i / (\sigma_i^2 + \lambda)$ — ridge shrinks every direction continuously while PCR either keeps or kills it. **Motivation:** multicollinearity inflates OLS variance in directions of small $\sigma_i$; discarding those directions stabilizes the estimate. **Critique:** component selection is *unsupervised* — it depends only on $X$'s variance structure, so directions with small $\sigma_i$ that happen to be highly predictive of $y$ are thrown away. Partial Least Squares (PLS) addresses this by choosing directions that maximize covariance with $y$, and supervised PCR (Bair et al., 2006) first screens covariates by marginal correlation with $y$ before applying PCR.

## Partial Least Squares Regression (PLS)

A regression method that, like PCR, projects the design matrix $X \in \mathbb{R}^{n \times p}$ onto a low-dimensional subspace before fitting — but chooses that subspace *supervised* by the response $y$. Where PCR's $k$-th direction $v_k$ maximizes $\mathrm{Var}(Xv)$ subject to orthogonality with earlier directions, PLS's $k$-th direction $w_k$ maximizes the squared covariance $\mathrm{Cov}(Xw, y)^2 = (w^\top X^\top y)^2$ subject to $\|w\| = 1$ and orthogonality to earlier score vectors. This fixes PCR's main weakness: a direction with tiny $\sigma_i$ but strong correlation with $y$ will be picked up by PLS and discarded by PCR. **Algorithms:** *NIPALS* extracts one component at a time by alternating between estimating a weight vector $w \propto X^\top y$, forming scores $t = Xw$, regressing $y$ on $t$, then deflating $X \leftarrow X - t p^\top$ with $p = X^\top t / \|t\|^2$ and deflating $y$ analogously; *SIMPLS* (de Jong, 1993) computes all components directly from the original (undeflated) matrices with orthonormal scores, giving identical predictions more efficiently. **Relationships:** with one component PLS reduces to a rescaled univariate regression on $X^\top y$; with $k = \mathrm{rank}(X)$ components PLS recovers OLS; it can be viewed as a compromise between OLS, PCR, and canonical correlation analysis (CCA maximizes correlation rather than covariance, so it ignores variance in $X$). **Uses:** chemometrics (spectroscopy, where $p \gg n$ and predictors are highly collinear), bioinformatics (gene expression vs. phenotype), neuroimaging, and sensometrics — broadly, ill-conditioned high-dimensional regression where the signal is thought to lie in directions that are neither high-variance in $X$ alone nor low-dimensional in $y$ alone but in the covariance between them.

## SVD Filtering

Given $A = U \Sigma V^\top$, replace each singular value $\sigma_i$ by $g(\sigma_i)$ for some filter function $g : \mathbb{R}_+ \to \mathbb{R}_+$ (or more generally $G : \mathbb{R}_+^k \to \mathbb{R}_+^k$ acting on the entire singular value vector), producing a modified matrix $\tilde{A} = U\, g(\Sigma)\, V^\top$. Solving $\min \|Ax - b\|$ through the filtered pseudo-inverse $\tilde{A}^\dagger$ yields implicit regularization — the choice of $g$ determines which regularizer. For example, $g(\sigma) = \sigma / (\sigma^2 + \lambda)$ recovers the Tikhonov-regularized solution to $\min \|Ax - b\|^2 + \lambda \|x\|^2$; more generally, $g(\sigma) = \sigma / (\sigma^2 + \lambda \sigma_\Gamma^2)$ where $\sigma_\Gamma$ are the singular values of a penalty matrix $\Gamma$ recovers the general Tikhonov solution $\min \|Ax - b\|^2 + \lambda \|\Gamma x\|^2$. Setting $g(\sigma) = 1/\sigma$ for the top $k$ values and $0$ otherwise gives truncated SVD. The framework unifies many classical regularization methods as different spectral filters on the same decomposition.

## Gaussian Mean Width

For a set $K \subseteq \mathbb{R}^n$, the **Gaussian mean width** is
$$w(K) = \mathbb{E}_{g \sim \mathcal{N}(0, I_n)}\!\left[\sup_{x \in K} \langle g, x \rangle\right],$$
where $g$ is a standard Gaussian vector. It measures the "size" of $K$ as seen from a random direction drawn from the Gaussian measure — the expected value of the support function $h_K(g) = \sup_{x \in K} \langle g, x \rangle$. For a symmetric set ($K = -K$) the definition is often written with a factor of $2$: $w(K) = \mathbb{E}[\sup_{x \in K} \langle g, x \rangle - \inf_{x \in K} \langle g, x \rangle]$. **Canonical examples.** The Euclidean ball $B_2^n$ has $w(B_2^n) = \mathbb{E}[\|g\|_2] \approx \sqrt{n}$; the $\ell^1$ ball $B_1^n$ (vertices $\pm e_i$) has $w(B_1^n) = \mathbb{E}[\|g\|_\infty] \approx \sqrt{2 \log n}$; a $k$-sparse set has $w \approx \sqrt{k \log(n/k)}$. **Why it matters.** The Gaussian mean width controls several high-dimensional phenomena. (i) *Compressed sensing / dimension reduction (Gordon's theorem):* if $m \gtrsim w(K)^2 / \delta^2$, a random Gaussian measurement matrix $\Phi \in \mathbb{R}^{m \times n}$ preserves the geometry of $K$ with distortion $\delta$ — the effective "statistical dimension" of the set is $w(K)^2$. (ii) *Covering numbers (Sudakov's inequality):* $\log N(K, \varepsilon B_2^n) \lesssim w(K)^2/\varepsilon^2$, bounding how many $\varepsilon$-balls are needed to cover $K$. The dual (Dudley's entropy integral) runs the other way: $w(K) \lesssim \int_0^\infty \sqrt{\log N(K, \varepsilon B_2^n)}\, d\varepsilon$. (iii) *Statistical learning:* the Rademacher complexity of a hypothesis class is controlled by (and often proportional to) the Gaussian mean width of the class via Gaussian–Rademacher comparison. **Relation to other widths.** The classical mean width $\bar{w}(K) = \int_{S^{n-1}} h_K(u)\, d\sigma(u)$ (uniform average over the sphere) and the Gaussian mean width agree up to a $\sqrt{n}$ factor: $w(K) = \sqrt{n/2\pi}\,\bar{w}(K)$ for origin-symmetric $K$, by rotational invariance and the surface-area formula. **Subadditivity and dilation:** $w(K_1 + K_2) = w(K_1) + w(K_2)$ (since $h_{K_1+K_2} = h_{K_1} + h_{K_2}$) and $w(\lambda K) = |\lambda| w(K)$; for a single point $w(\{p\}) = \mathbb{E}[\langle g, p \rangle] = 0$ since $\mathbb{E}[g] = 0$, so $w$ measures spread, not location.
